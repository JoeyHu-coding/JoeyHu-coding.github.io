Research Agenda

Human-settlement environments at the interface of Earth systems and urban systems.

My research asks how planetary processes, urban form, infrastructure expansion, land development, and environmental governance jointly shape the environments in which people live.

Overall Positioning

Urban-Earth system coupling through human-settlement environments

Cities occupy a limited share of land, but they concentrate population, infrastructure, energy demand, and environmental exposure. They cannot be understood apart from Earth systems: solar radiation, surface-energy balance, climate disturbance, land-cover change, and ecological processes all enter urban life through built form, infrastructure, land use, and policy.

I therefore use human-settlement environments as the central interface. This framing keeps the work broader than a narrow city boundary, while still focusing on the everyday environmental conditions, risks, and welfare consequences experienced by people.

Central Question

How do Earth-system processes and urban-system transformations interact across scales, and how can those interactions be measured, evaluated, and governed to improve human-settlement quality?

Interface
Human-settlement environments
Objects
Light, infrastructure, land, habitats, and climate risk
Approach
Remote sensing, GeoAI, spatial modeling, and policy analysis
Theme 01

Current core contribution

Urban Light Environments: Daylight, Shadow, and Day-Night Light Cycles

My current work focuses on urban light environments as a direct link between Earth-system rhythms and urban three-dimensional form. Daylight and shadow are produced by planetary motion and solar geometry, but their human-settlement consequences are mediated by buildings, street canyons, urban morphology, and land-surface context.

Global sunlight and shadow dataset

I am developing one of the first global-scale datasets that characterizes sunlight and shadow conditions within three-dimensional urban space. It describes how points in cities experience sunlit or shaded conditions over a full annual cycle.

Day-night light cycles

Building on daytime sunlight and shadow information, I integrate nighttime-light data to study complete day-night light-environment dynamics and their spatiotemporal patterns across human settlements.

Settlement quality and exposure

This line supports questions about urban form, environmental exposure, spatial inequality, health-relevant conditions, and how light environments shape everyday human-settlement quality.

Theme 02

Governance and emerging infrastructure

Environmental Policy and Emerging Infrastructure: Data Centers

I use data centers as an entry point for connecting environmental measurement with policy implementation. As one of the fastest-growing infrastructure systems, data centers are reshaping electricity demand, clean-energy requirements, land development, water use, carbon emissions, and local environmental burdens.

My research examines their environmental footprint and population-relevant impacts, then links those impacts with renewable-energy supply, power-system constraints, climate background, and policy measures. This theme extends my work from identifying environmental patterns toward evaluating governance pathways for new infrastructure.

Theme 03

Ecological space and settlement welfare

Green Exposure, Land Use, and Habitat Quality

This theme builds from my earlier research foundation in green-space exposure and landscape ecology. Rather than treating green space as an isolated amenity, I study how land development and spatial planning transform ecological-space patterns, and how those transformations affect human green exposure, habitat quality, ecological welfare, and environmental equity.

The work connects land-use change, urban expansion, landscape configuration, habitat quality, green-gray imbalance, accessibility, and planning-oriented evaluation. It provides an ecological and spatial foundation for my broader agenda on human-settlement environments.

Theme 04

Extreme disturbance and adaptation

Climate Risk, Infrastructure Resilience, and Human-Settlement Quality

My climate-risk work expands from earlier heat-environment research toward compound risks affecting human settlements. These include heat exposure, flooding, wildfire, drought, tropical cyclones, and the ways multiple hazards increasingly overlap in space and time.

The central concern is not a list of hazards, but whether infrastructure systems and settlement environments have enough resilience to maintain safety, functionality, and livability under climate stress. This theme connects environmental exposure with infrastructure vulnerability, adaptation capacity, and human-settlement quality.

Methodological Integration

Remote sensing, GeoAI, computer vision, deep learning, and large language models

Methods are not a separate research object in this agenda. I use them as an integrated toolkit for observing, modeling, predicting, and interpreting urban-Earth system interactions and their policy implications.

Environmental Remote Sensing

Observes land-surface conditions, urban form, green space, land-surface temperature, nighttime lights, and environmental exposure across scales.

Computer Vision

Extracts three-dimensional urban morphology, spatial structure, built-environment features, and visual environmental information from imagery.

GeoAI and Deep Learning

Infers geographic features, fills spatial-data gaps, detects complex spatial patterns, predicts environmental conditions, and supports risk modeling.

Large Language Models

Supports policy-text analysis, environmental-policy synthesis, literature mining, and evidence aggregation for governance-oriented research.
